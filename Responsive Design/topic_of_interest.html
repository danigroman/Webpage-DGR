Journalism

Journalism is important to me because it allows me to protect narratives, tell stories, and ensure that cultural memory is preserved.
Growing up in a Cuban household, I’ve seen firsthand how my culture and history have been tarnished by false and censored reporting.
From the Cuban government state reporting, tourists and the world are often misled by false statistics and news, hiding the truth of death marches, labor camps, political prisoners, famine, and firing squads.

This experience has made me acutely aware of the power and responsibility of journalism.
I am passionate about news gathering because it enables me to uncover and share these truths, as well as connect with people around the world.
I believe that every story, no matter how small, far away, or different, deserves to be told.
By reporting accurately and honestly, I can help ensure that these important stories are not forgotten and that people understand the real situation.

My background in journalism has equipped me with the tools to uncover and share these stories.
I am committed to giving a voice to those who might otherwise be silenced and making sure that cultural memories are not erased due to lack of reporting.
For me, journalism is about more than just presenting facts; it’s about making sure that the true stories are remembered.
I find it crucial to protect narratives because they shape our understanding of the world and our place in it. By telling stories, I can challenge misinformation and provide a more accurate and nuanced view of different cultures and histories.

Los rescatistas trabajan contra reloj para sacar a los mineros atrapados en una mina inundada en México

Diez mineros llevan más de 24 horas atrapados en una mina de carbón inundada en el norte de México, mientras los rescatistas luchan por llegar a ellos.

El incidente se reportó este miércoles por la tarde, cuando los mineros se encontraron con un túnel abandonado con agua que inundó la mina, dijeron las autoridades.

Los rescatistas lograron extraer a cinco personas de la mina el miércoles, y ahora están trabajando para bombear el agua fuera del área inundada, según la Coordinadora Nacional de Protección Civil, Laura Velázquez, quien está supervisando la operación de rescate.

Al menos 10 mineros quedaron atrapados en mina en Coahuila y cinco fueron rescatados

"No hemos dormido, estamos trabajando día y noche, sin interrupción", dijo Velázquez este jueves.

"Estamos colocando bombas estratégicamente en cada uno de estos pozos para poder extraer la mayor cantidad de agua y tener acceso inmediato a las minas y rescatar a los mineros lo antes posible", dijo.

"Dicen que el agua está subiendo... Quiero que mi marido salga bien", dijo a Reuters la esposa de un minero atrapado, Erika Escobedo.

Escobedo dijo a la agencia de noticias que había pasado "toda la noche viendo los esfuerzos de rescate en el lugar".

El gobernador del estado de Coahuila y Zaragoza, Miguel Riquelme, viajó el jueves al municipio de Sabinas donde se encuentra la mina.

"Al momento trabajan cinco bombas de extracción de agua que suman 150 caballos de fuerza y se están instalando otras bombas sumergibles, que están enviando empresas de la región y así continuar con las labores de rescate", escribió en Twitter.

Sabinas no es ajeno a las tragedias mineras. En 2006, una explosión en una mina local terminó con la vida de 65 personas. En 2011, 14 mineros quedaron atrapados y se confirmó su muerte tras la explosión de otra mina en Sabinas.

Según un comunicado emitido por la Secretaría de Trabajo y Previsión Social de México, la mina en la que actualmente están atrapados los diez mineros comenzó a operar en enero, y "no se tiene antecedentes de denuncias por algún tipo de anomalía".

En su rueda de prensa diaria de este jueves, el presidente Andrés Manuel López Obrador dijo que cualquier investigación sobre la mina llegaría solo después de que concluyera la operación de rescate.

"Los responsables, los permisos, las inspecciones, todo, todo eso, lo estamos dejando para después. Ya tenemos la información básica", dijo.

"Pero no hablemos de eso ahora, busquemos salvar a los mineros".

Peru PM resigns as investigations target President Castillo

Peruvian Prime Minister Anibal Torres Vasquez has abruptly resigned, becoming the fourth premier to leave the job in the past year.

Torres announced his resignation in a letter to President Pedro Castillo on Wednesday, attributing his decision to “personal reasons” and wishing his “friend” Castillo success.

“I retire from this position after having served alongside you, our homeland (and) particularly the put-off and forgotten people,” said Torres’ letter, which he posted on Twitter.

By Peruvian law, Castillo must accept or reject his resignation.

Castillo told local media on Thursday that a new cabinet will be sworn in on Friday.

“From today I will make some decisions regarding the Cabinet, tomorrow, we are going to swear in this Cabinet, and I hope it will be a Cabinet that follows our call….. Let’s create a broad-based Cabinet to work for Peru,” Castillo said while being constantly interrupted by reporters.

Torres, whose tenure lasted almost 6 months, announced his resignation almost a week after President Castillo marked one year in power.

He accepted the role in February, after former Prime Minister Hector Valer stepped down amid allegations of domestic violence against him.

Valer, who had been in office just for four days, denied the allegations.

Torres’ resignation now comes as Castillo himself is under pressure to resign by the opposition. Castillo is currently the subject of five investigations, four of them for alleged corruption.

On Thursday, Castillo went to the prosecutor’s office along with his legal team to testify on one of those allegations.

Castillo has previously admitted he had made mistakes and said he was willing to cooperate with any investigation.

“I present myself to justice in order to clarify the charges that are attributed to me, with respect to the due process and not to media justice,” Castillo said during a speech before Congress celebrating Peru’s National Day on July 28.

According to Peru’s constitution, a sitting president may only be impeached on four charges: treason; preventing presidential, regional or local elections; dissolving Congress; or blocking the work of the National Election Jury or other electoral bodies.
